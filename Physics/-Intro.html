What is Physics?

So, what is physics? Physics is defined as the science that deals with the structure of matter and the interactions between the fundamental constituents of the observable universe. [1]

The word physics comes from the Latin physica ('study of nature'), which itself is a borrowing of the Greek φυσική (phusikḗ 'natural science'), a term derived from φύσις (phúsis 'origin, nature, property'). [2][3][4] The study of Physics boomed in the 17th century with the start of the scientific revolution. This was where famous scientists like Galileo Galilei, Isaac Newton, and Johannes Kepler made their discoveries.

Today, physics is a broad and complex field that encompasses many areas of study. Some of the major branches of physics include classical mechanics, quantum mechanics, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, and relativity. Each of these branches deals with different aspects of the physical world and has its own set of laws and principles. Physics is a fundamental science that underpins all other branches of science and has applications in many different fields, from engineering and technology to medicine and environmental science.

Why do we study Physics?

Why do we study physics? Physics is the most fundamental of all the sciences. It deals with the basic principles that govern the behavior of the universe, from the smallest particles to the largest galaxies. By studying physics, we can gain a deeper understanding of the world around us and the forces that shape it. Physics is also a highly practical science, with many applications in technology, engineering, and medicine. By studying physics, we can develop new technologies, improve existing ones, and solve some of the most pressing problems facing society today. In short, physics is the key to unlocking the mysteries of the universe and harnessing its power for the benefit of humanity.
